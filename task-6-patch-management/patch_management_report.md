**Introduction**



Modern organisations depend on computers, applications, databases, mobile devices and network equipment to carry out their daily work. The software running on these systems is not always perfect. Developers may discover security weaknesses, technical errors or performance problems after the software has already been released.



Software vendors respond by releasing patches and updates. Installing these updates helps organisations correct known problems before attackers can take advantage of them. However, an organisation may have hundreds or thousands of devices, which makes it difficult to know what needs to be updated and whether every update was installed successfully.



Patch management provides an organised way to handle this responsibility. In this report, I explain what patch management is, why it is important, the consequences of failing to apply patches and the practices organisations can use to improve their patching process.



**1. What is a software patch?**



A software patch is an update released to correct or improve an existing product. A patch may fix a security vulnerability, correct a technical problem or improve the way a system operates.



Patches can apply to different technologies, including:



\- Operating systems

\- Web browsers

\- Mobile applications

\- Business software

\- Database systems

\- Web servers

\- Routers and firewalls

\- Internet of Things devices

\- Firmware

\- Cloud services



A patch is not always a complete replacement for the software. It normally changes only the parts needed to correct a particular problem.



**Types of patches and updates**



**Security patches**



A security patch corrects a weakness that could allow unauthorised access, information exposure or another form of attack. These patches are important because details about a vulnerability may become publicly available after the patch is released.



**Bug fixes**



A bug fix corrects a problem that affects the normal operation of software. A bug may cause an application to crash, produce an incorrect result or use resources badly.



**Feature updates**



Feature updates introduce new functions or improve existing ones. They may also include security and reliability fixes.



**Firmware updates**



Firmware is software built into hardware such as routers, printers, storage devices and security cameras. Firmware updates may correct security problems or improve the way the hardware works.



**Emergency patches**



An emergency or out-of-band patch is released outside the vendor's normal update schedule. This normally happens when a serious vulnerability requires urgent attention.



**Cumulative updates**



A cumulative update combines several previous fixes into one package. This makes it easier to bring an older system up to date.



**2. What is patch management?**



Patch management is the process used to identify, obtain, test, prioritise, install and verify software updates.



According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology, enterprise patch management includes identifying, prioritising, acquiring, installing and verifying patches, updates and upgrades across an organisation.



Patch management is therefore more than clicking an update button. An organisation needs to know which systems it owns, which software versions are installed, which vulnerabilities affect those systems and whether the updates were successful.



A good patch-management process should answer questions such as:



\- What devices and applications does the organisation use?

\- Which systems are affected by a new vulnerability?

\- How serious is the risk?

\- Is the vulnerability being actively exploited?

\- Could the patch cause compatibility problems?

\- When should the patch be installed?

\- Who is responsible for installing it?

\- Was the installation successful?

\- What should happen if the patch fails?



**3. Why patch management is important**



**It corrects known security vulnerabilities**



Attackers often target vulnerabilities that are already publicly known. When a vendor releases a security update, the vulnerability does not disappear automatically. Every affected organisation still needs to install the update.



A system that remains unpatched may continue to be vulnerable even though a solution is already available.



**It reduces the risk of malware**



Malware can use software vulnerabilities to enter a system, spread across a network or gain additional permissions. Applying patches reduces the number of known weaknesses that malware can exploit.



**It protects sensitive information**



Organisations may store customer details, employee records, payment information and confidential business documents. An unpatched vulnerability could provide a way for an attacker to access or change that information.



**It supports system availability**



Some vulnerabilities allow attackers to crash systems or interrupt services. Patching can prevent these problems and reduce unexpected downtime.



**It supports legal and regulatory responsibilities**



Organisations are expected to take reasonable steps to protect information. Failing to address a serious known vulnerability may contribute to legal action, regulatory penalties or compensation claims after a breach.



**It improves reliability and performance**



Not every patch is related to security. Some updates correct errors, improve stability or resolve compatibility problems. Keeping important software updated can therefore improve both security and normal system performance.



**It helps maintain customer trust**



Customers expect organisations to protect their information. A breach involving a vulnerability that already had an available patch can make people question whether the organisation handled security responsibly.



**4. Consequences of failing to apply patches**



An ineffective patch-management process may result in:



**Unauthorised access**



A known vulnerability may allow someone to enter a system without permission or obtain access beyond what they should have.



**Data breaches**



Attackers may gain access to personal, financial or business information. The organisation may then have to investigate the incident, notify affected people and provide support.



**Malware and ransomware**



Unpatched systems can provide an entry point for malware. Ransomware may interrupt operations by making important systems and information unavailable.



**Business interruption**



A cyber incident can stop employees from working and prevent customers from using services. Recovery may take hours, days or longer, depending on the seriousness of the incident.



**Financial loss**



**The costs may include:**



\- Technical investigation

\- System recovery

\- Legal advice

\- Regulatory penalties

\- Customer support

\- Lost sales

\- Security improvements

\- Compensation for affected people



**Reputational damage**



Customers and partners may lose confidence in an organisation after learning that a preventable security weakness contributed to a breach.



**Compatibility and support problems**



Very old software may eventually become incompatible with newer systems. Vendors may also stop providing technical assistance and security updates for unsupported products.



**5. Real-world example: WannaCry**



WannaCry was a ransomware attack that spread widely in May 2017. It used a vulnerability affecting the Windows Server Message Block service.



Microsoft had released security update `MS17-010` on 14 March 2017, before the widespread WannaCry attack. The update addressed serious vulnerabilities in Windows, including the weakness later used by WannaCry.



Microsoft warned that computers without the update were at greater risk. Systems with the appropriate update were protected against the vulnerability used by the attack.



**This incident demonstrates several patch-management lessons:**



1\. A patch may be available before large-scale exploitation begins.

2\. Organisations need a way to identify affected systems quickly.

3\. Critical patches should be prioritised.

4\. Old and unsupported systems create additional risk.

5\. Organisations must verify that important patches were installed successfully.



The lesson is not simply that updates should be installed. Organisations need a reliable process that ensures urgent updates reach every affected system.



**6. Real-world example: Equifax**



Equifax experienced a major data breach in 2017 that affected approximately 147 million people. The incident involved a vulnerability in Apache Struts, a framework used by one of the company's systems.



According to a complaint published by the United States Federal Trade Commission, Equifax received information about the vulnerability and sent an internal instruction requiring responsible employees to apply the patch. However, the affected system remained unpatched.



The FTC later announced a settlement of at least $575 million, with the possibility of the total reaching $700 million. The settlement was connected to allegations that Equifax had failed to take reasonable steps to secure its network.



This incident shows that sending an email about a patch is not enough. A successful patch-management process also requires:



\- An accurate record of affected systems

\- Clear responsibility

\- Tracking of patch deployment

\- Verification after installation

\- Escalation when a critical patch is missing

\- Regular vulnerability scanning



The Equifax case demonstrates how a failure in patch management can contribute to large financial, legal and reputational consequences.



**7. The patch-management process**



An organisation can use the following process to manage patches.



**Step 1: Maintain an asset inventory**



The organisation should keep an updated list of its devices, operating systems, applications, versions and responsible owners.



It is difficult to patch a system if the organisation does not know that it exists.



**Step 2: Monitor for new patches**



Security teams should monitor trusted sources, including:



\- Software-vendor notifications

\- Security advisories

\- Vulnerability databases

\- CISA alerts

\- The CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities Catalogue



This helps the organisation learn about new threats and available fixes.



**Step 3: Assess the risk**



The organisation should determine:



\- Whether the vulnerability affects its systems

\- Whether attackers are actively exploiting it

\- Whether the system is accessible from the internet

\- What information or service is at risk

\- How serious the possible impact would be



**Step 4: Prioritise**



Critical vulnerabilities affecting important or internet-facing systems should normally receive attention before low-risk problems on isolated systems.



CISA recommends using its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities Catalogue when prioritising vulnerability-management work. A vulnerability with evidence of active exploitation may require faster action.



**Step 5: Test the patch**



Where possible, the organisation should test the patch on a small group of devices or in a controlled environment. Testing helps identify compatibility and performance problems before wide deployment.



**Step 6: Create backups and a recovery plan**



Important information and configurations should be backed up before major changes. The organisation should also know how to reverse the update if it creates a serious problem.



**Step 7: Deploy the patch**



The patch should be installed according to its priority and the organisation's maintenance schedule. Emergency patches may require faster deployment.



**Step 8: Verify the installation**



The organisation should check whether the patch was installed successfully. A failed or incomplete update should not be recorded as complete.



Verification may involve checking software versions, update records, device status or vulnerability-scan results.



**Step 9: Document and monitor**



The organisation should record:



\- The affected system

\- The patch installed

\- The installation date

\- The responsible person or system

\- Whether installation succeeded

\- Any problems that occurred

\- Any system still awaiting an update



Monitoring should continue after deployment to identify unexpected problems.



**8. Patch-management best practices**



**Keep an accurate inventory**



An asset inventory should be updated whenever systems are added, changed or removed. It should include third-party applications, not only operating systems.



**Assign clear responsibility**



Every important system should have an identified owner. Employees should understand who approves, tests, deploys and verifies patches.



**Use risk-based prioritisation**



Patches should be prioritised according to actual risk. Active exploitation, severity, system exposure and business importance should all be considered.



**Automate routine work**



Automated tools can identify missing patches, deploy approved updates and report failures. Automation reduces manual work, but the results should still be monitored.



**Test before wide deployment**



Testing helps prevent one faulty update from affecting every system. A small group of devices can receive the patch first.



**Establish an emergency process**



The organisation should have a faster process for vulnerabilities that are actively exploited or could cause serious damage.



**Verify every important patch**



The process should not end when deployment begins. Reports should confirm that installation was successful and identify systems that failed.



**Remove unsupported software**



Software that no longer receives security updates should be replaced, upgraded or isolated. Continuing to use unsupported software creates long-term risk.



**Use temporary protections when necessary**



Sometimes an update cannot be installed immediately because of compatibility or operational concerns. Temporary measures may include restricting network access, disabling an affected feature or increasing monitoring.



These controls reduce risk but should not become permanent replacements for patching.



**Review the process**



Organisations should measure how quickly important patches are installed, how many installations fail and how many unsupported systems remain. These results can be used to improve the process.



**9. Challenges of patch management**



Patch management can be difficult for several reasons.



A new patch may not work correctly with an older application. Some important systems cannot easily be restarted during business hours. Remote devices may be offline when updates are deployed. Legacy systems may use software that is no longer supported.



Large organisations may also have thousands of devices managed by different teams. Without a central inventory and clear responsibilities, some systems may be missed.



This is why patch management should balance security and operational needs. Delaying every patch because of possible disruption is unsafe, but installing every update immediately without testing may also create problems.



A risk-based process allows urgent vulnerabilities to be addressed quickly while less critical updates follow a planned schedule.



**10. Benefits of effective patch management**



An effective patch-management programme can provide the following benefits:



\- Fewer known vulnerabilities

\- Reduced risk of malware and data breaches

\- Improved system reliability

\- Less unexpected downtime

\- Better compliance

\- Clearer responsibility

\- Improved visibility of technology assets

\- Faster response to serious vulnerabilities

\- Continued vendor support

\- Greater customer and partner confidence



Patch management does not remove every cybersecurity risk. New vulnerabilities may exist before a patch becomes available, and attackers may also use stolen passwords or social engineering. However, patching removes many known weaknesses and makes systems more difficult to compromise.



**Conclusion**



This research helped me understand that patch management is an ongoing cybersecurity responsibility, not a once-off activity. Installing updates is only one part of the process. Organisations must also identify their systems, monitor for vulnerabilities, assess risk, test updates, verify installation and document the results.



The WannaCry and Equifax incidents show what can happen when known vulnerabilities remain unpatched. In both cases, security updates were available, but unpatched systems remained exposed.



An effective patch-management process reduces the risk of data breaches, malware, downtime and financial loss. It also improves reliability and helps organisations maintain the trust of their customers.



The most important lesson is that a patch is only useful when the organisation identifies the affected system, installs the update and confirms that the vulnerability has been addressed.
